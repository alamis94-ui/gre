## Province Description

Sicily, the largest island in the Mediterranean, was colonised by Greeks from the later eighth century BCE, with Syracuse becoming the largest and most powerful Greek polis; in time most of the indigenous settlements of the interior also became profoundly Hellenized. The western tip of the island, however, was settled by Phoenicians and later became an outpost of Carthaginian control. The clash between the superpowers, Rome and Carthage, in the third century BCE meant that Sicily became embroiled in the First Punic War which resulted in Rome’s acquisition of the island as its first overseas provincia in 241 BCE; but the wealthy and extensive kingdom of Hieron II, based on Syracuse but extending over much of eastern and south-eastern Sicily, was allowed to remain independent, becoming absorbed into the Roman province only in 211 BCE Sicily was above all famed for its agricultural fertility and its capacity to produce exceptional grain yields (wheat and barley), and to a lesser extent wine. By the time of the Roman Empire the principal flourishing cities were located on the coast, with much of the interior given over to scattered agricultural villages, villas and farms.

The study of ancient gardens in Sicily is still in its infancy: archaeological excavation of them has barely commenced. That already in classical Greek times there was an interest in enhancing the natural ambience by the careful planting of trees and the creation of public open spaces is hinted at by the ancient sources. A notable early experiment, for example, was the construction in the fifth century BCE of an artificial lake, the Kolymbetra, in the south-west quarter at Akragas (Agrigento), full of fish and a haven for swans and other birds; according to Diodorus (11.25.4; 13.82.5), who stresses the pleasure it gave to the people of Akragas, the lake had a perimeter of seven stades (ca. 1250 m). It has traditionally been identified with the sunken area near the ‘Temple of Vulcan’ once called the Giardino della Badia, but although a sixteenth-century report refers to remnants of walling (now disappeared) which were interpreted as dams, it is far from clear that the area in question could have been made to contain water. Wherever its precise location, the Kolymbetra was a self-confident assertion of the power and prosperity of a Sicilian Greek city during the rule of its beneficent tyrant, Theron (ca. 489–473 BCE), and a clear demonstration of the city’s precocious technical expertise. Later, Diodorus reports (11.25.4), it was filled in and planted with ‘vines and trees of every description placed close together’, but now the area was earmarked for commercial profit, and no longer as a pleasure-park for the people of Akragas.

Religious precincts were doubtless another area of any Greek city with the potential for providing shade in the form of planted gardens, but archaeologically they have yet to be identified in Sicily. Thucydides (6.99), however, noted that the Temple of Apollo Temenites in Syracuse had trees, since the Syracusans had to cut them down to build their defensive counterworks against the Athenians in the crisis of 415 BCE Aelian (De natura animalium 11.3) reports that the precinct of the temple of Hephaistos at Aetna-Inessa, a town so far unidentified on the south-west slopes of mount Etna, also contained ‘sacred trees’. Also from the literary sources come occasional glimpses of secular gardens: Plato’s second letter (Ep. 2.313A) refers to a garden exclusively of laurel trees which he saw in Sicily in the 360s BCE, almost certainly in the royal palace at Syracuse on Ortygia; the garden there was divided into various sections by walls. Athenaeus (12.542A) also mentions the garden in this palace where the king received audiences; he says that it was called the ‘Muthos’ (‘The Word’). He also states (ibid.), quoting Callias’ now lost History of Agathocles (and so referring to the early third century BCE), that the ‘entire region around Panormos (Palermo) is called a garden because it is full of cultivated trees’; but this may be a reference to the fertile area south-west of the city that the Arabs were later to call ‘The Golden Conch’ (Conca d’Oro), rather than to a self-contained garden in the normal sense of the word.

In private housing in both town and country, no excavation has so far been attempted to reveal the presence of gardens, and little is known of the arrangements for running water in houses to irrigate such gardens or to supply ornamental pools and fountains. Ornamental pools are known in four domus gardens. The precise dates of all of them are uncertain. Villa gardens in Sicily have also attracted little archaeological attention. Four villas have ornamental pools of differing sizes in their peristyles.
