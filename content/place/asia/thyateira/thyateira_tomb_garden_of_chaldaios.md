## Garden Description

According to a marble plaque of Trajanic or Hadrianic date found near Akhisar, “Fabius Zosimus, prepared a sarcophagus for himself and his dearest wife Aurelia Pontiana and put it in a place free of graves in front of the city on the side of the shrine of Sambatha (a local Sibyl) in the garden (*peribolo*) of Chaldaeus (or “the Chaldaean”) beside the public road” (*Φάβιος Ζώσιμος κατασκευάσας σορὸν ἔθετο ἐπὶ τόπου καθαροῦ ὄντος πρὸ τῆς πόλεως πρὸς τῶι Σαμβαθείωι ἐν τῶι Χαλδαίου περιβόλωι παρὰ τὴν δημοσίαν ὁδὸν ἑαυτῶι ἐφ’ ᾧ τεθῇ καὶ τῆι γλυκυτάτηι αὐτοῦ γυναικὶ Αὐρηλίᾳ Ποντιανῆι*)<!--https://epigraphy.packhum.org/text/264573?hs=919-925-->. Here, as often elsewhere in central and western Anatolia, a tomb garden seems to be referred to metonymically by reference to its perimeter wall. 

The establishment of a tomb monument in a garden belonging to an extraneous party is found elsewhere in Asia Minor at Hierapolis in the complex of M. Aurelius Valerianus and Cl(audius) [N]ausimachus. The date and location of Fabius’s funerary disposition is confirmed by a subscription to his declaration, carved on another face of the stone, which places it ”in the most splendid city of Thyateira” (*ἐγένετο ἐν τῆι λαμπροτάτῃ Θυατειρηνῶν πόλει*)<!--https://epigraphy.packhum.org/text/264573?hs=919-925--> during the consulship (probably the first consulship) of L. Catilius Severus, who held the office twice, in 110 and 120 CE.
