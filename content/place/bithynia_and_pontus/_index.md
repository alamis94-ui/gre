## Province Description 

The province of Bithynia and Pontus lay in northwest Anatolia, between the Bosporus and the lower Sangarius River, and extended from the southern coast of the Black Sea to the area of Lake Ascania and the Kapıorman Dağları mountain range.  Although mountainous, like most of the territory of modern Turkey, the river valleys of this northern coastal area afforded fertile plains and adequate communication routes.  After the local sovereign Nicomedes died in 75 BCE, Rome annexed the kingdom of Bithynia and Pompey incorporated it as a province in 63 BCE, combined with the region of Pontus in northern Anatolia along the coast of the Black Sea.  This area was infamous as the home of Mithridates VI, the formidable enemy to Rome in the late second and early first centuries BCE.  Under Marcus Antoninus Pius, Bithynia and Pontus obtained the status of an imperial province.  Between these areas was Paphlagonia, another ancient territory of the Black Sea region, which was administered as part of Bithynia and Pontus during the Roman period.

As a Roman province, cities in Bithynia such as Nicomedia and Apamea, as well as large estates in the area of Nicaea, became prominent.  In Pontus, sites including Sinope, Comana and Neocaesarea also flourished and in Paphlagonia, cities such as Germanicopolis and Pompeiopolis added to the region’s imperial value.  The survey results of Project Paphlagonia, carried out in the southern part of this coastal territory in the 1980s, revealed new dimensions in settlement patterns during the Roman period as well as the identification of Paphlagonian sites such as Hadrianopolis and Antoninopolis.  As a result, settlement appears to have been widespread across almost the entire landscape, and there is evidence for a hierarchy of size, ranging from large town to small hamlet.  No garden archaeology, however, has been performed in this region.

Bithynia was economically one of the most important provinces of the Roman empire and there is epigraphic and archaeological evidence for numerous imperial estates and land holdings.  A corpus of extant letters between Trajan and Pliny the Younger (who served as proconsul in Bithynia) from the early second century CE also testify to Bithynia’s contemporary elite culture.  Primarily an agriculturally-rich region, producing fruits and grains, the territory’s forests and marble quarries were also critical to the Roman economy.  Olives were grown throughout the Pontic region, as mentioned by Strabo (12.3.30), and olive presses have been found around the site of Comana Pontica.  Interestingly, this later Roman-era exploitation of the olive contrasts with Xenophon’s (*Anab*. 6.4.6) observations in the late fifth century BCE for the lack of olives grown along the Bithynian coast.   Strabo (12.3.15) also notes the abundance of grapes, fruits and nuts in orchards in the Pontic region, at sites such as Amisus on the coast.  Horace (*Odes* 1.14.11-12) and Strabo (12.3.12) note that Bithynian pine was used as timber in the shipbuilding industry, and Virgil (*Georgics* 2.437-8) describes the local boxwood.    In one of Pliny the Younger’s letters to Trajan (10.70), he mentions seeing an estate with a garden in the city of Prusa, despite what Woolf has noted as his reluctance to describe the area and his overall generalization of the Bithynian landscape.  Strabo (12.3.30) mentions that Mithradates Eupator, one of the descendants of Mithridates the Great, had a palace at Cabeira in Pontus with gardens and hunting grounds.  Aside from these literary references, however, there has been no archaeological attention to gardens in the region to date.
