## Dates

Late first century CE until the fourth century CE.

## Garden Description

This farm, known as Hambach 59, was built in the late 1st century CE and inhabited until its destruction in the 4th century (Plan 1). The farmhouse, 19.50 x 26 m. in size (A on plan), occupied a position in the northern part of the complex, and looked out on the rest of the farm to the south where various outbuildings and stables stood. There was no division between the residential part and the service area of the farm.

The farmyard was enclosed by a hedge (dotted lines on plan) and took up an area of approximately 1.5 hectares. Outside of the enclosed farmyard to the north were four large plots, presumably for orchards and cultivated fields, which were marked off by ditches or fences (outlined in black on plan). Two of these plots were approximately one hectare in size. The reconstruction of the vegetation on the farm is based on the palaeobotanical analysis of deposits from the four wells in front of the house. The material includes charred fruits and seeds as well as unburnt plant remains and pollen. Cherry pits are evidence for sweet cherry trees [(Prunus avium)](https://www.gbif.org/species/3020791). Oak pollen, as well as the water-logged remains of the buds and bud scales of the oak tree, suggest that a shady oak [(Quercus sp.)](https://www.gbif.org/species/2877951) may have grown near one of the wells in the courtyard. Walnut pollen was found in such large quantities in the wells, that one can safely assume the presence of walnut trees [(Juglans regia)](https://www.gbif.org/species/3054368) near the house. The same applies for lime trees [(Tilia sp.)](https://www.gbif.org/species/3152041) attested by their pollen in the sediments. The remains of [(Coriandrum sativum)](https://www.gbif.org/species/3034871) and linseed [(Linum usitatissimum)](https://www.gbif.org/species/2873861) in the wells suggest that these plants may have grown in the vegetable garden in the immediate vicinity of the house. The hedge surrounding the farm compound may have consisted of a combination of hazelnut [(Corylus sp.)](https://www.gbif.org/species/2875967), elderberry [(Sambucus sp.)](https://www.gbif.org/species/2888721), raspberry and brambleberry [(Rubus fruticosus)](https://www.gbif.org/species/8436459), which were all present as pollen, shells, and seeds in the sediments. Box [(Buxus sempervirens)](https://www.gbif.org/species/2984671) is attested by the pollen in the wells, but it is uncertain where this plant grew on the site.
